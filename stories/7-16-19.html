A Snippet in the Mindset of a Runner Up

How a Tennis Legend deals with it

“Did Federer really take Nadal out in the semi-finals of Wimbledon?!” I asked one of my friends whom I play tennis with every now and then. He then proceeds to send me a highlight video hosted by ESPN, and of course, I had to watch it. First off, good work on ESPN with these highlights, the superb video quality and actual coverage of the match is levels beyond those given by in the past years by official grand slam YouTube channels. Second, wow. The rallies between the two players were immense. Seeing the explosive movements from both players contrasted by the damping of the grass made it seem as if they were moving so gracefully, yet from my experience, I have an idea of how much energy it takes to move that quick. A lot.

After the highlights, I asked my friend when the finals were: Sunday. Who? Djokovic vs Federer. A few days pass, the match finishes, and he sends me the highlights. Damn. As a Federer fan, I must say, that must’ve hurt. A five-hour match ending in the longest way possible, the fifth-set tiebreak—a new rule at Wimbledon. Again, great quality highlights. Sadly, the results were in the title of the video, but of course, I had to watch it regardless. A close first set lost by Federer in the tiebreak, returning to win the second quickly, only to lose another tiebreak in the third, only to win another set in the fourth by a break—and then, in final set, with Federer having two match points to win it, Djokovic perseveres and brings Federer to the 12-all tiebreak and closes it out.

Damn. I sat there and thought: “I have to watch the post-match interviews. How does a world class athlete handle such a loss?” Luckily, there’s a stream on YouTube. I laid there in bed and listened as Federer explained how he felt about the two match points he could have won to take the championship and about his loss:

“No, not really. I was still happy to be at 8-all, 9-all? You try to see the positives, you try to take it as a good thing—you’re not down a break, or the match is not over yet…You just always try to push yourself to see things on the better side, but yes it was definitely tough to have those chances…You take it on your chin. You move on, and you try to forget and try to take the good things out of this match—there’s tons of it. And…think, ‘well it’s not that bad’ but for now, it hurts, and it should…I think I’m very strong at being able to move on. I don’t want to be depressed about an actually, amazing tennis match.”

Listening to his words, his tone, and seeing his body language, it’s apparent that Federer feels the pain of losing such a close match, as he, himself, clearly stated above. But also apparent is Federer’s mindset on losing. Despite the pain, his mindset is one of trying to see the positives in the shortcomings. When he lost the points that could’ve have won him the match, it was better than being down a break, and when he finally lost it, it was better to take the good things from the match and appreciate it for what it was—and it was—an amazing match. It seems like the next time I fail, I’ll have to look at the takeaways as well.
